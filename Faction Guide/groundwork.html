Faction framework

This companion volume reads faction cards, tactical cards, and units through the same plain-English-first method as the core rules guide. It is not a replacement for the core rules. It is the layer that makes faction content easier to learn, easier to compare, and easier to rule cleanly.
How to use this book
Read every entry in three passes: plain-English summary first, practical role and synergy notes second, and RAW watch boxes only when wording is likely to create friction at the table.
What this book is doing

It carries over the tone and structure of the core guide so the faction material does not feel like an appendix dump. Every major card type gets a readable explanation, battlefield role notes, and medium-depth matchup advice.

What this book is not doing

It is not quoting RAW everywhere. RAW is used intentionally and selectively where wording, timing, placement, or exceptions are likely to matter.

Faction tag eligibility
RAW says

Every card in the game features Faction Tags. A Faction Card always displays a Race Tag and usually includes a Sub-Faction Tag as well. When building an army, every tag on the Unit or Tactical Card must also appear on the Faction Card. If even one tag does not match, that card cannot be included in the army.

Plain English

Read legality from the card that is trying to get in, not from the faction card. A card only needs all of its own tags to be present on the chosen faction card.

Table effect

A single extra tag can disqualify a card even if the rest match. A card with fewer tags than the faction card is still legal.

Tactical cards and army slots
RAW says

Tactical Cards unlock additional Army Slots beyond those provided by the Faction Card. Tactical Cards may be purchased with Vespene Gas to increase the number of available slots. If a Tactical Card is marked as Unique, only one copy may be included in the army.

Plain English

Faction cards give the starting frame. Tactical cards are how you widen that frame before the game begins.

Table effect

Do not judge a list only by its faction card. Check its tactical infrastructure too.

Resources, costs, and card states
RAW says

Each faction has a separate resource type to spend on the faction’s abilities: Terran uses CP, Zerg uses BM, and Protoss uses PE. To pay for an ability’s cost, the player must Exhaust a Ready card that matches the resource type. If the player cannot Exhaust enough cards to pay the entire cost, they cannot activate the ability. Excess resources are lost.

Plain English

Resources are generated by exhausting real cards. They are not a pooled currency you bank between effects.

Table effect

A card can either stay ready, get exhausted for its own ability, or get exhausted to pay for something else. It cannot do both at once.

Special ability timing
RAW says

An ability may be used only when all its requirements are met. The timing or trigger condition must be correct, and any Phase restrictions must be satisfied. All Costs must be paid in full before the ability resolves, and the Unit using the ability must be on the battlefield.

Plain English

Faction and tactical abilities do not float free of the turn structure. They still obey timing, target legality, and payment rules.

Table effect

When a faction card looks powerful, first ask when it is allowed, who it can affect, and what has to be exhausted to pay for it.

Protoss resource naming mismatch

Why this matters: the core rules describe Protoss faction resource as PE, while the expanded Protoss faction presentation shows EN on card faces. This guide keeps the source visible and flags the mismatch instead of silently normalising it.

RAW watch

Core rules: “Protoss uses PE.”

Faction presentation: Protoss faction and tactical cards display “+1 EN.”

Guide reading: treat this as a source inconsistency that should be agreed at the table before play. Do not let one player use the printed face and the other use the core resource label as if they were different systems.
