Zerg field guide

Zerg

Board pressure, tempo swings, and recursive positioning. Zerg thrives when the table starts to feel crowded and your opponent has to answer multiple angles at once. Use bodies, spawn effects, creep, burrow, and alternate entry points to stretch the board until the opponent starts making reactive instead of proactive moves.
Why this race needs a guide layer
Zerg has the highest density of wording-sensitive effects in this faction set: creep states, burrow, structures, extra entry edges, replacement units, and return-to-reserves interactions.
Read this race by layers

Start with the faction cards to understand the army frame. Then read the tactical cards as infrastructure. Only after that should you read the unit cards, because many unit evaluations change once the right support shell exists.

This guide's method

Every entry gets a plain-English read first. Selective RAW watch boxes appear only where wording is likely to matter at the table.

Faction Card

Kerrigan's Swarm

Faction Card
Faction CardFaction CardUnique

Resource +1 BM; Slots: Hero slot 1, Elite slot 2, Core slot 3.

Plain-English reading

Kerrigan's Swarm sets the army frame first and the play pattern second. It gives +1 BM when exhausted and opens Hero 1, Elite 2, Core 3. Its key faction tools are Wild Mutation (Active); Zerg Creep (Passive).

Role in the army

Force-multiplier faction card with strong board-position leverage.

Ability summary
  • Any Phase — Wild Mutation (Active): If the active unit is ON CREEP , the Unit gains BUFF Speed (1) and its first Weapon used gains PRECISION (1).
  • Any Phase — Zerg Creep (Passive): During Army Building , select exactly one Creep Card and add it to their Army List, paying its listed cost (if any).
  • Movement Phase — Omega Network (Active): If there is no Friendly Omega Worm on the battlefield, place a Friendly Omega Worm Unit anywhere on GROUND LEVEL of the battlefield, more than 10" away from any Enemy model. This Round, the Omega Worm is not eligible to use its Special Abilities (excluding Structure).
Strengths
  • changes positioning or deployment in ways raw statlines cannot
  • gets much better when its faction state engine is online
Watch-outs
  • needs the board to compress before it gets full value
Synergy notes
  • Needs creep pieces or creep-extending cards nearby to stay above rate.
Matchup watch
  • Protoss: Force the elite army to answer too many problems at once. Protoss hates spending premium activations on cleanup instead of decisive pushes.
  • Terran: Break Terran support posture before the gunline settles. Recursive entry edges, creep, and pressure pieces matter more than pretty trades.
  • Zerg: Board control and infrastructure are everything. In the mirror, the side that manages entry points, creep, and disposable bodies better usually writes the tempo.

Zergling reads as a core piece that leans on support text as much as raw statline. In melee it usually relies on Claws with RoA 2 and damage 1. Its defining rule text is Squadron (Passive), Metabolic Boost (Active (1 Biomass)).

Role in the army

Forward pressure or melee-finisher piece that wants committed fights.

Matchup watch
  • Protoss: Force elite units to spend energy or support effects answering you early. Protoss hates trading premium activations for cheap board-control chores.
  • Terran: Break Terran support posture before the gunline settles. Recursive entry edges, creep, and pressure pieces matter more than pretty trades.
  • Zerg: Commit fast enough to matter. Half-measures let Zerg bodies trade for time while their infrastructure keeps paying them back.

How to study this race well
  • Read the faction cards first. They tell you what kind of game the list wants to play.
  • Read tactical cards second. They explain which slot pressure, resource pressure, and infrastructure tricks the race can actually support.
  • Only then read unit cards. A unit's real value often depends on whether the support shell described above is present.
  • Use RAW watch boxes only when the plain-English summary is not enough to settle a table question.
