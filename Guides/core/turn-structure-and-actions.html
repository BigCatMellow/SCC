Race, Faction, Slots, Mustering, and Upgrades
Army legality starts with identity. Choose a race and faction first, then read slots, mustering, and upgrades through that choice.
Page role
List Legality

This page is the legality check for force construction: identity, slots, starting supply, and upgrade purchase timing.

Read for
Building Rule Vs. Live Rule

Keep starting-supply mustering separate from current-supply battlefield logic, because they answer different questions.

16.1 Choose Race And Faction
Rule text

Pick one race:

  • Terran
  • Zerg
  • Protoss
  • Then choose exactly one faction card from that race.
16.2 Slots
Rule text

Your faction card gives starting slots such as:

  • Core
  • Elite
  • Support
  • Air
  • Hero
  • Tactical cards can add more slots.
16.3 Mustering Units
Rule text

A unit occupies a number of slots equal to its starting supply value. This is an army-building rule, not a live battlefield calculation.

16.4 Upgrades
Rule text

Purchase upgrades during army building. If a specialist upgrade changes one model’s weapon, that model may later resolve attacks as a separate batch.

16.5 Summoned Units
Rule text

Summoned units are governed by the summoning rules and source text. They are not generally bought the same way as normal roster units.

16.6 Team Games
Rule text

Each player still builds independently, even in team formats, unless a more specific scenario rule says otherwise.

RAW references: Part 9.1.2, 9.1.5-9.1.9.
Lore note

Army building in StarCraft is a question of identity before it is a question of efficiency. Terran forces mix resilience and combined arms, protoss forces lean on elite quality and psionic mastery, and zerg broods overwhelm with adaptation, pressure, and bodies. A legal list is also a statement about what kind of commander you are fielding.

Draft Control Matters

Who controls which draft changes the shape of the game long before the first activation happens.

Overview
Section summary

The mission and deployment are chosen through a short draft.

17.1 What Each Player Brings
Rule text

Each player brings two mission cards and two deployment cards, with no duplicates in their own set.

17.2 Roll-Off Decisions
Rule text

The winner of the roll-off chooses:

  • whether they are the Red or Blue player
  • whether they control the Mission Draft or the Deployment Draft.
  • The opponent controls the other draft.
17.3 Draft Procedure
Rule text

For missions:

  • the player without mission-draft control removes any 2 mission cards
  • the player with mission-draft control chooses 1 of the remaining 2
  • the final card is discarded.
  • Then repeat the same structure for deployment cards.

After the draft:

  • confirm table dimensions from the deployment card

Across the Koprulu Sector, battles are shaped by ruined colonies, industrial strongholds, temple worlds, creep-infested wastelands, and desperate frontier defenses. Setup rules matter because StarCraft combat is almost never abstract: terrain, approach lanes, and deployment conditions decide who can exploit speed, firepower, or swarm pressure first.

Game Length and Phase Order
This page gives the game its tempo. It tells you how long the battle can last, when each phase happens, and how initiative moves from one phase to the next.
Page role
Game Rhythm

This page is the timing frame for the rest of the book: rounds, phases, alternating activation, and passing.

Read for
Phase First

Many later disputes become simpler once you identify the exact phase and activation state before judging the action itself.

Overview
Section summary

A game lasts up to 5 rounds unless a mission says otherwise.

Each round has four phases:

  • Movement
  • Assault
  • Combat
  • Scoring & Cleanup
19.1 Activation System
Rule text

During phases 1, 2, and 3, players use alternating activation. The holder of the First Player Marker decides who activates first each phase.

An activated unit performs exactly one available action for that phase.

19.2 Passing
Rule text

A player may pass instead of activating a unit. A player must pass if they have no legal unit left.

The first player to pass in a phase gains the first-player marker for the next phase. Once a player passes, they cannot activate further units in that phase.

19.3 Determining The First Player In Round 1
Rule text

Players roll off. The winner assigns the first-player marker to either player for round 1.

RAW references: Parts 8.1-8.2, 12.2.

IX.01
Part IX
Reserves and the Supply Pool
Reserves and on-table units follow different rules. The supply pool is the gate that determines what can arrive and when.
Selective RAW lens
Lowercase “instant” is not the INSTANT keyword
RAW says
“The Supply Pool is the total available capacity that any commander may have at any instant …”
Plain English

That sentence is ordinary English. It is describing the pool as a live capacity, not invoking the INSTANT glossary keyword.

Table effect

Do not import weapon-keyword rules into the Supply Pool section just because the same word appears there in lowercase.

Page role
Entry Logic

This page explains who is on the table, who is still waiting, and what supply allows to arrive right now.

Read for
Capacity And Restriction

Treat the supply pool as live deployment capacity, not just a general sense of how much army you own.

Overview
Section summary

No units begin on the battlefield.

All units in the army list start in reserves.

20.1 Supply Pool
Rule text

The mission sets:

  • starting supply in round 1
  • supply escalation each later round.

In the final round, the supply pool becomes unlimited and remaining reserve units may be deployed without supply restriction, following normal alternating activation.

20.2 Available Supply
Rule text

Available supply = current supply pool - total current supply of friendly units already on the battlefield

A unit can only be deployed if its current supply value is less than or equal to available supply.

20.3 Hard Cap
Rule text

Your total current on-table supply may never exceed your supply pool.

20.4 Deployment Restriction
Rule text

A unit arriving from reserves may not end that arrival inside the opponent’s zone of influence.

RAW references: Parts 8.3-8.4, 6.2, 12.3.
RAW clarification note

The word "instant" in the Supply Pool section is ordinary English. Do not read it as the INSTANT keyword from the glossary. The sentence just means the pool is the total capacity available at any moment.

IX.02
Part IX
Movement Phase Actions
The Movement Phase is where legal position is built before later pressure begins. Each action here changes what the Assault and Combat phases will allow next.
Worked ruling
Disengage failure has two different failure points

If the Leading Model cannot clear all enemy Engagement Ranges, the action fails, the unit does not move, the Leading Model is removed, and the activation ends. If a non-leading model cannot be placed outside all enemy Engagement Ranges, remove that model and continue resolving the rest of the unit.

Overview
Section summary
  • Deploy
  • Hold
  • Move
  • Disengage
21.1 Deploy
Rule text

A unit in Reserves enters the battlefield through the Entry Edge using the standard move procedure:

  • confirm that the unit's Current Supply Value fits within Available Supply
  • set the Leading Model in contact with the player's Entry Edge outside the battlefield
  • then move that model up to Speed following the normal movement rules
  • set the rest of the unit into coherency
  • the unit may not end that activation inside the opponent's Zone of Influence
21.2 Hold
Rule text

Do nothing. The unit still counts as activated for the phase.

21.3 Move
Rule text

An unengaged unit repositions using the standard move procedure:

  • choose a leading model
  • move it up to Speed
  • rebuild the rest of the unit in legal coherency.

Movement restrictions include:

  • no passing through enemy models
  • no passing through friendly models from other units
  • no ending within 1" of an enemy ground unit
  • no moving through size 2+ terrain
  • no elevation changes except through access points
  • no overlapping bases or impassable terrain.
21.4 Special Note on Other Models in the Unit
Rule text

After the leading model moves, other models are set, not moved. They may ignore intervening obstacles while being repositioned, but they must still end in a legal place.

21.5 Disengage
Rule text

If an engaged unit disengages in Movement:

it moves out of combat

and in the following Assault Phase it cannot make a ranged attack or charge unless its own supply is greater than the enemy combined supply involved in the disengage check.

This is one of the places where battlefield supply has immediate tactical consequences.

RAW references: Parts 8.4-8.5, 6.2, 12.3.
Lore note

Mobility is one of StarCraft’s defining asymmetries. Terrans reposition through discipline and mechanized flexibility, protoss strike with deliberate speed and superior technology, and zerg flood space through numbers, instinct, and biological acceleration. Movement rules carry that identity by making space, formation, and timing decisive before attacks even begin.

RAW clarification note

The source Deploy text contains one broken duplicate sentence around the Entry Edge instruction. Read the rule as: the Entry Edge functions as the starting line for the Leading Model, then the model moves up to Speed under the normal movement rules.

RAW clarification note

RAW separates failure states. If a non-leading model cannot finish strictly outside all enemy Engagement Ranges, remove that model and continue with the rest. If the Leading Model itself cannot clear all enemy Engagement Ranges, the Disengage fails, the unit does not move, the Leading Model is removed, and the activation ends.

Quick check
When a movement argument starts, ask this first
Path

Is this standard path movement, or a set effect like PLACE that skips pathing entirely?

Legality

Even if the route is legal, can every model still end in a legal final position without overlap?

Assault Phase Actions
The Assault Phase turns position into threat. Units either improve position, commit to engagement, or begin resolving ranged pressure.
Page role
Threat Menu

This page is the Assault Phase action menu that turns position into immediate pressure through running, charging, or shooting.

Read for
Which Action Is Actually Legal

Before resolving the action, make sure the unit is allowed to declare that kind of pressure at all.

Overview
Section summary

In the Assault Phase, a unit on the battlefield with the correct activation state may:

  • Run
  • Hold
  • Charge
  • Ranged Attack
  • Units still in reserves cannot act in this phase.
22.1 Run
Rule text

Run follows the same standard movement procedure as a Move.

22.2 Hold
Rule text

Do nothing. The unit still counts as activated.

22.3 Charge
Rule text

Move into engagement with one or more declared enemy units using the charge procedure.

22.4 Ranged Attack
Rule text

Use ranged weapons to attack a valid target using the ranged attack procedure.

Lore note

In StarCraft fiction, battles often turn on the first committed strike: a marine firing line, a zealot crash into the front, or a zerg surge that reaches contact all at once. The Assault Phase captures that moment where positioning becomes immediate threat and hesitation turns into lost tempo.

RAW clarification note

Multi-charge is all-or-nothing. The charge succeeds only if the Leading Model can legally end within Engagement Range of every declared target and outside the Engagement Range of every undeclared enemy unit.

Base-to-base contact is the first placement priority whenever it is physically possible. If that is impossible, the unit must still end within Engagement Range of the declared targets.

X.02
Part X
Ranged Attack Structure
This is the full ranged-attack sequence from target declaration to damage. Read it as an ordered procedure, not a loose summary of how shooting usually works.
Worked example
Legal target, illegal dice contribution

A unit may declare a legal target, but only the models that are both Visible and Within Range actually contribute attack dice to the batch.

Worked example
Engagement can kill the shot before range does

If the attacker is unengaged and the target unit is engaged, the attack can already be illegal before you ever start counting inches.

Overview
Section summary

A ranged attack happens in two broad stages:

  • choose a valid target and assign weapons
  • resolve the attack through the three-pool system.
23.1 Target Eligibility
Rule text

A target must satisfy all relevant conditions:

  • visible
  • within range
  • valid combat-tag target for the weapon
  • legal under engagement restrictions.
  • Only models that are both visible and within range contribute attack dice.
23.2 Engagement Targeting Rule
Rule text

An unengaged attacking unit cannot target an engaged unit, except where template-weapon spillover rules say otherwise. An engaged attacking unit may only target the unit or units it is engaged with.

23.3 Split Fire
Rule text

If a unit has multiple relevant weapon profiles, it may split fire into separate batches.

RAW clarification note

An unengaged attacker cannot target an engaged unit except where the template spillover rules create a specific exception. An engaged attacker may make ranged attacks only against the enemy unit or units it is already engaged with.

RAW clarification note

This restriction is based on the attacker’s current engagement state. An Engaged unit may still make a Ranged Attack, but only against the unit or units it is already Engaged with. A weapon keyword can then be stricter than that baseline: for example, BULKY forbids that specific weapon from being used while the unit is Engaged at all.

Check yourself • If one model in the unit can see the target, do all models add RoA?

No. Visibility and range are checked at the contributing-model level. A legal target declaration does not make the whole unit automatically contribute dice.

X.03
Part X
The Three-Pool Attack System
The three-pool system shows where attack dice actually go as the sequence unfolds. The pools matter because different rules move dice at different steps.
Tracking tip
Do not collapse the pools together

Attack Pool decides which hit dice survive. Armour Pool decides which of those are stopped. Damage Pool is where unsaved damage and bypass effects finally sit together.

Attack PoolArmour PoolDamage PoolEvade Step
Check yourself • Where does Evade happen?

At the Damage Pool step, after armour and after any effects that move dice directly into damage.

Return to The Three-Pool Attack System ↑
Overview
Section summary

Every attack moves dice through:

  • Attack Pool
  • Armour Pool
  • Damage Pool
24.1 Attack Pool
Rule text

Generate dice equal to RoA from eligible attacking models. Roll against the weapon’s Hit. Successful dice move to the Armour Pool. Failures are discarded.

24.2 Surge And Critical Movement Into The Damage Pool
Rule text

Some rules, especially Surge and CRITICAL HIT, move dice directly out of the Armour Pool into the Damage Pool. Those dice bypass armour entirely.

24.3 Armour Pool
Rule text

The defender rolls armour for dice in the Armour Pool.

  • successes are discarded
  • failures move to the Damage Pool.
24.4 Evade Step
Rule text

Evade is not automatic. The defender only rolls Evade if the target qualifies for an Evade roll.

A target qualifies if:

  • a rule or keyword grants an Evade roll, or
  • it is engaged and is suffering damage from a ranged attack.
  • Successful Evade dice are removed from the Damage Pool.
24.5 Damage Application
Rule text

Each die that remains in the Damage Pool inflicts the weapon’s damage value.

24.6 Null Evade Still Matters
Rule text

A unit with Evade - cannot make an Evade roll even if some effect would otherwise grant one, unless a more specific rule explicitly overrides that limitation.

RAW references: Parts 8.7.3-8.7.4, 3.4, 3.6, 5.1, 12.4.
RAW clarification note

The Evade step happens after Armour Rolls and after any dice moved directly into the Damage Pool by Surge or CRITICAL HIT. Timing keywords such as ANTI-EVADE apply at this step, not at Hit or Armour.

Common mistake
Surge and critical movement do not skip the later damage step

Those dice bypass Armour by moving into the Damage Pool early, but they are still part of the normal damage-resolution timing once they are there.

X.04
Part X
Charge
A charge is a movement action with combat obligations attached. Declared targets, rolled distance, and legal end position all matter before the charge can count as successful.
Selective RAW lens
A multi-charge must satisfy every declared target
RAW says
“The Charge succeeds only if the Leading Model can be legally set in a position that is … Within the Engagement Range (1\") of every declared Charge target … [and] not Within the Engagement Range of any Enemy Unit that was not declared as a target.”
Plain English

Charge is not partial-success movement. The final position either satisfies all declared targets and all legality checks, or the charge fails.

Table effect

For multi-charge, reach every declared target legally and avoid undeclared ones. If no such final position exists, the unit does not get a “best effort” charge.

Check yourself • Can a multi-charge partially succeed?

No. This guide treats multi-charge as all-or-nothing. The final position must satisfy every declared target requirement.

Return to Charge ↑
Overview
Section summary

A charge is the Assault Phase action that moves a unit into engagement.

Charge uses movement logic but with combat-specific destination requirements:

  • the unit must move to engage its declared target
  • models should be placed in base-to-base where possible
  • if base-to-base is not possible, they must be placed within engagement range where possible
  • and then arranged as close as possible to the leading model while maintaining coherency.
  • A charging unit cannot use the move to engage new enemy units that are not part of the declared charge target structure.

XI.02
Part XI
Close Combat Attack
This section explains how melee is resolved once engagement exists. It covers who can fight, how Close Ranks works, and why casualty removal is tighter here than in ranged attacks.
Page role
Melee Procedure

This page explains how an engaged ground unit actually resolves combat once the phase reaches it.

Read for
Melee Is Not Ranged

Do not import ranged assumptions here. Close Ranks, engagement integrity, and casualty priority are stricter.

Overview
Section summary

A close combat attack is the Combat Phase procedure for an engaged ground unit.

27.1 Close Ranks
Rule text

Before attacking, the unit may make a Close Ranks reposition:

  • choose a leading model
  • move it up to 3" under standard move logic
  • but it must end closer to the enemies it was already engaged with
  • and it cannot use this movement to engage new enemy units.

Then:

  • reposition the rest of the unit into coherency
  • set models into base-to-base wherever possible
  • otherwise into supporting legal positions.
  • Models already in base-to-base at the start of this step are pinned and do not move.
27.2 Who Can Attack
Rule text

The engaged unit attacks enemy units it is engaged with.

27.3 Casualty Priorities In Melee
Rule text

Melee casualty removal uses a stricter priority system than normal visibility-based casualty handling. The source gives a priority order that protects engagement integrity:

  • remove models not within engagement range of any enemy unit
  • then remove models within engagement range but not in base contact
  • then remove models in base contact.

Where there is another legal casualty available, the defender cannot remove a model in a way that would improperly break engagement with a specific enemy unit.

This is a major advanced-user rule. In melee, casualty choice is more constrained than many players expect.

RAW references: Part 8.8, Part 12.5, casualty priority text in Part 8.
Lore note

Close combat in StarCraft is brutal and symbolic. Protoss warriors like zealots are defined by disciplined melee ferocity, zerg organisms tear into enemies through sheer biological violence, and terrans usually enter melee only when pressure has already collapsed the line. The Combat Phase therefore feels less like skirmishing and more like a decisive break in the battle.

RAW clarification note

Close Ranks is optional for normal units, but if you choose it, it is a real reposition. The Leading Model must actually end in a legal new position that is closer to the enemy units it was already engaged with. A zero-inch non-move does not satisfy the "must end closer" requirement.

Models already in base-to-base at the start of Close Ranks are pinned and do not move. Close Ranks also cannot be used to engage a new enemy unit.

Part XII
Mission-Marker Control
Marker control is a legal state, not just a matter of being nearby. Distance matters, but so do line of sight, elevation, coherency, and eligible supply.
Selective RAW lens
Marker control is eligibility first, supply second
RAW says
“A Unit may Contest a Mission Marker only if all three conditions are met …” and “A Unit at Supply 0 may still Control a Marker if no Enemy Units contest it.”
Plain English

You do not start by counting supply. First ask whether the unit is legally eligible to contest at all. Only then do you compare current supply totals.

Table effect

Supply 0 still matters in an uncontested state. Tied supply does not flip control, and once a marker is claimed it stays claimed until the opponent retakes it with a higher eligible total.

Worked ruling
Supply 0 can still matter

A supply-0 unit loses head-to-head comparisons, but it can still control a marker when no enemy unit is legally contesting it. Supply 0 means weak control, not no control.

Overview
Section summary

A unit can contest a mission marker only if:

  • it is on the battlefield
  • at least one model is within 3" of the marker
  • that model has line of sight to the marker
  • that model is on the same elevation as the marker
  • and the unit is in coherency.
  • The marker is treated as Size 0 for LoS purposes.
29.1 Units That Cannot Control
Rule text

Flying units can never control or contest mission markers.

29.2 Supply Decides Control
Rule text

Add up the current supply value of each eligible contesting unit. The player with the higher total controls the marker.

If the totals tie:

  • the marker is contested
  • control does not transfer.
29.3 Sticky Control
Rule text

Once a marker is controlled, it remains under that player’s control until the opponent ends a later scoring phase with a higher eligible contesting supply total. A contested result does not neutralize the marker. A marker cannot revert to neutral once claimed.

29.4 Supply 0 And Control
Rule text

A supply-0 unit may still control a marker if there is no enemy unit contesting it.

RAW references: Parts 8.9.1, 6.2, 12.6.
Lore note

Wars in the sector are fought for more than casualties. Command nodes, warp sites, extraction points, xel’naga ruins, reactor yards, and strategic crossroads all matter. Scenario control rules reflect the fact that commanders in StarCraft win by securing critical objectives, not only by destroying whatever stands in front of them.

RAW clarification note

Supply 0 does not mean "cannot control." RAW only says Supply 0 loses head-to-head comparisons. If no enemy unit is contesting the marker, a Supply 0 unit can still control it.

Decision ladder
Marker control in the right order
Step 1

Check battlefield presence, distance, line of sight, elevation, and coherency.

Step 2

Remove units that are Flying or otherwise ineligible.

Step 3

Add current supply only for the units still left in the contest.

Step 4

Higher eligible total controls. A tie leaves the marker contested and does not neutralize sticky control.

Victory Points and End-Of-Game Checks
This section explains when points are awarded and when the game ends. It also separates immediate win conditions from the ordinary end-of-round and end-of-game checks.
Page role
Win-State Logic

This page explains when the game ends immediately and when it continues to final scoring.

Read for
End Condition Before Tiebreaker

Check whether the battle has already ended before arguing about final VP or mission tiebreakers.

Overview
Section summary

Both players score based on the mission card.

The game ends immediately if:

  • a special winning condition is fulfilled
  • a player has no models on the battlefield and no units in reserves
  • or the final phase of the final round is complete.

If the game reaches the round limit:

  • score mission VP
  • in the final scoring phase, all units still in reserves count as destroyed
  • then determine the winner by VP
  • use the mission tiebreaker if one exists
  • otherwise the game is a draw.

XIII.02
Part XIII
Active Abilities
Active abilities are chosen effects with strict timing. Their power comes from timing discipline, not from being declared whenever they would be most helpful.
Selective RAW lens
“Immediately after” means after the whole action
RAW says
“The ability must be triggered either immediately before declaring an action or immediately after fully resolving one. It cannot be used during an action.”
Plain English

“After” is not a pause inside a charge, a ranged-attack sequence, or a casualty step. It means after the action and its compulsory substeps are finished.

Table effect

Resolve the action completely first. Then declare the Active Ability, unless a more specific rule explicitly opens a special timing window mid-action.

Common misread
"Immediately after" does not mean "pause the middle of the action"

Players often try to slide an Active Ability into the middle of a charge, attack sequence, or casualty step. RAW treats that as too late for "before" and too early for "after" unless a more specific rule opens that timing window.

Overview
Section summary

Active abilities:

  • require the unit to be currently activated
  • must be used immediately before declaring an action or immediately after fully resolving one
  • cannot be used during the action itself
  • usually expire at the end of the round unless stated otherwise
  • cannot be used in reserves unless the rule says otherwise.
32.1 Frequency
Rule text

A specific unit may use a named active ability only once per round, unless it has REPEATABLE. This is per unit, not global.

If the same active ability is granted by a tactical card, the once-per-round restriction still applies to that unit.

RAW clarification note

The timing window is strict. An Active Ability must be used either immediately before declaring an action or immediately after fully resolving one. It cannot be inserted during an action unless the source specifically overrides that rule.

Immediately after fully resolving one means after the whole action is over, including compulsory substeps created by that action. You cannot pause inside a Charge, Ranged Attack sequence, casualty step, or Close Combat sequence to declare an Active Ability unless a more specific rule explicitly creates that timing window.

Timing drill
Ask these before declaring an Active Ability
Window

Am I truly before the action, or after the whole action is fully resolved?

Eligibility

Is the unit activated and actually on the battlefield right now?

Cost

Can I pay the full cost immediately, with the correct resource source?

Frequency

Has this unit already used that named Active Ability this round?

Passive abilities do not wait for a separate declaration. They are simply on when their conditions are met, which makes order and simultaneity the real reading problem.

Part XIII
Reaction Abilities
Reaction abilities live in narrow trigger windows. The real skill here is recognizing the exact moment the window opens and closes.
Timing discipline
A reaction window is a moment, not a whole attack

If the trigger is “when targeted,” the reaction belongs to that exact targeting moment. Once the game moves forward into later attack steps, that window is gone.

Overview
Section summary

Reaction abilities:

  • occur at a defined trigger point
  • must be declared exactly when that trigger occurs
  • cannot be used retroactively if the window is missed
  • usually expire at end of round unless stated otherwise
  • cannot be used from reserves unless explicitly allowed.
34.1 Frequency and reaction limit
Rule text

A specific unit may use a named reaction only once per round.

Each player may resolve only one reaction per activation.

If both players want to react to the same trigger:

  • the active player resolves first
  • then the opponent resolves.
  • This is one of the most important timing disciplines in the game.
RAW clarification note

The reaction limit is per player, per activation. If both players want to react to the same trigger, resolve the Active Player's reaction first and the opponent's second. A single unit also remains bound by its own once-per-round reaction limit.

A reaction window belongs to the trigger sentence, not to the whole attack or activation. If the trigger is “when targeted,” declare before resolution advances into later attack steps. Once the game moves past that exact moment, the window is missed.

Quick check
Resolve reaction arguments with these two questions
Window

Did the trigger just happen, or is the table trying to declare the reaction after the game already advanced?

Limit

Has that player already used their one reaction for this activation, or has that unit already used that named reaction this round?

Part XIII
Tactical Cards, Faction Cards, and Card States
Once faction and tactical cards are in play, their state matters. Ready versus exhausted, face-up versus face-down, and payment versus effect all carry rules weight.
Page role
Card Resource Engine

This page explains how faction and tactical cards behave once they are in the game and how exhaustion turns into payment.

Read for
Right Now, Not Banked

Resources here are spent in the moment. They do not float forward as stored value for later use.

Overview
Section summary

Faction and tactical cards can provide:

  • special abilities
  • a faction resource when exhausted.
35.1 Resource types
Rule text

The core rules use:

  • CP for Terran
  • BM for Zerg
  • PE for Protoss.
35.2 Ready and exhausted
Rule text

Cards are either:

  • Ready (face up)
  • Exhausted (face down).

You may exhaust a ready card either:

  • to use that card’s own ability
  • or to generate its resource to pay for another ability elsewhere.
35.3 Paying costs
Rule text

If you cannot exhaust enough matching cards to pay the full cost, you cannot use the ability. Excess generated resource is lost immediately. Resources cannot be banked or transferred to another ability.

35.4 Refresh timing
Rule text

Exhausted cards are refreshed during Phase 4 cleanup.

35.5 Source note on Protoss resource naming
Rule text

The rules text in Parts 5 and 10 uses PE for Protoss resource naming, while the faction source cards visually present Protoss faction resources as EN. This guide preserves the rules text as written and flags the discrepancy for table awareness.

RAW references: Parts 10.1-10.5, Part 5.3-5.4, faction source files.
RAW clarification note

End-of-Round effects resolve before Cleanup & Refresh. Temporary effects that last until the End of the Round remain active while those End-of-Round effects are being resolved, and are removed during Cleanup, not earlier.
